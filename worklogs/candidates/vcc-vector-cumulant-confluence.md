VCC (Vector-Cumulant Confluence) indexes experience by the running per-channel vector cumulant `c_t = Σ_{s≤t} v_s` (quantized into buckets) rather than by observation-hash (the approach that killed FED, SIT, RSD). For each (bucket, action) pair it maintains the empirical continuation-cumulant Pareto front `F(b,a)` — the set of Pareto-non-dominated `c_T − c_t` deltas observed from that context — and at decision time nudges policy logits by the cross-action dominance margin (count of points in one action's front that strictly dominate points in other actions' fronts, minus vice-versa). The structural argument for density is sound: the cumulant accumulates at every step so buckets fill naturally even on long-horizon sparse envs, unlike obs-hash buckets which are sparse by definition.

The first panel run (vector stage, 120s) produced below-random performance on both vector envs (Deep Sea Treasure: 99.0 vs random 194.0; Resource Gathering: 0.011 vs random 1.331). The method ran to completion with no implementation errors. The likely culprit is untuned quantization (bucket explosion to singletons — no continuations to compare, dominance margin always 0) or bucket collapse to a single cell (margin uninformative). Before re-running, the implementation needs diagnostic instrumentation: bucket-occupancy histograms after each episode, mean Pareto front size per bucket, and fraction of decision steps where the dominance margin was non-zero. Without those diagnostics the engineer is blind to whether the structural idea is actually engaging at all. Once diagnostics confirm the primitive is engaging, α tuning and quantization-granularity search are the levers. The scalar-reward rebadge risk on envs with k=1 also needs an audit: if all continuations in a bucket are totally ordered (no Pareto-incomparable points), VCC is conditional return-to-go on that env and must be flagged.
