# 20260605-03-auto — Reconvergent Segment Dominance (RSD)

## Research Gate

primitive: a directed multigraph over observation-hash nodes whose parallel edges are vector-tagged action segments extracted from past trajectories
improvement_operator: for any node pair `(u, v)` with two or more parallel edges, behavior-clone the policy toward the Pareto-dominant edge (by accumulated within-segment vector) with strength proportional to the count of edges it dominates
side_information: transition geometry (observation-hash equivalence on visited states) + vector diagnostics (per-step or per-segment vector channels, including step cost when no other channel exists)
nearest_prior_or_disqualifier: #15 SIT (Suffix-Inheritance Trie) — alive-weak, in `worklogs/candidates/sit-suffix-inheritance-trie.md`
falsifier: on the vector envs (Deep-Sea Treasure, Resource-Gathering) RSD must accumulate at least one parallel-edge pair within the panel time budget *and* show a measurable Pareto frontier shift on the panel's `info["vector"]` channel; on DoorKey-8x8 it must beat random; if all observation-pair edge multiplicities stay at 1 across the run, the primitive has nothing to operate on and the candidate is dead.

## Mechanism

A trajectory `τ = (o_0, a_0, o_1, a_1, ..., o_T)` is decomposed into all contiguous segments `s_{i→j} = (o_i, a_i, ..., a_{j-1}, o_j)` together with the segment's accumulated vector signal `g(s_{i→j}) ∈ ℝ^k` (sum of per-step vector feedback over the segment, with step count appended as a baseline channel so the primitive is well-defined under terminal-only reward). Each segment becomes a parallel edge from node `h(o_i)` to node `h(o_j)` in a multigraph `G`, where `h` is an observation-hash function. The primitive is the per-node-pair **edge multiset** `E(u,v) = { g(s) : s is a segment from u to v }`. The improvement operator inspects every `(u, v)` with `|E(u,v)| ≥ 2`, computes the Pareto frontier of `E(u,v)` under coordinate-wise dominance, picks any frontier edge `s*` and any strictly-dominated edge `s_-`, and applies a behavior-cloning gradient step on the policy toward the action sequence of `s*` conditioned on the trajectory context up to `o_i`, weighted by `|{s ∈ E(u,v) : s* ≻ s}|`. No critic, no scalar reward weight, no return-to-go, no rollout verification — the dominance witness is intrinsic to data already collected, and the action-sequence target is on-distribution because `s*` is something the agent actually executed.

## Required candidate shape

1. **Experience object:** standard trajectories `(o_t, a_t, vec_t)_{t=0..T}` from on-policy interaction, with `vec_t` the per-step vector feedback (channels: success, cost, safety, ...; on terminal-only reward we append a constant `1` step-cost channel so the primitive is non-degenerate).

2. **Core primitive:** the per-node-pair edge multiset `E(u, v)` of accumulated vector signals over all observed action segments going from observation-hash `u` to observation-hash `v`. Equivalently, a sparse mapping `(u, v) ↦ {(action_seq_k, vec_k)}` populated by streaming trajectory decomposition. *Not* `Q(s, a)`, *not* a return distribution, *not* a value baseline, *not* a flow `μ_π(s, a)`, *not* a goal-conditioned policy. It is a witness store of *natural counterfactuals* between identical observational endpoints.

3. **Improvement operator:** for each `(u, v)` with `|E(u,v)| ≥ 2`, compute coordinate-wise Pareto dominance on `E(u, v)`. For each (Pareto-dominant `s*`, Pareto-dominated `s_-`) pair, take a single supervised-learning gradient step `∇_θ log π_θ(a^{s*}_t | context_t)` summed over the dominant segment's actions, weighted by the dominance count. Update is purely supervised; nothing references reward magnitude — only the partial order. There is no Bellman backup, no reward-weighted log-prob; the only quantity entering the policy gradient is *the count of segments dominated by `s*`*, which is order-information, not reward-magnitude information.

4. **Execution rule:** the policy `π_θ(a | o, context)` samples actions during data collection. There is no explicit greedy phase: as the multigraph fills with witnesses, the cloning updates implicitly bias the policy toward Pareto-dominant transitional segments. A small temperature schedule on softmax sampling ensures continued segment diversity (so new parallel edges keep arriving).

5. **Vector feedback rule:** vector channels are *never collapsed*. Pareto-dominance is computed coordinate-wise over the raw `k`-dim accumulated vector. Two segments are incomparable iff neither dominates the other in every channel — incomparable segments yield zero updates, preserving Pareto-optimal diversity in the policy. If the panel env reports `info["vector"]`, every component of it is treated as an independent dominance dimension; no fixed `w` is applied at any point.

6. **Rollout-cost discipline:** one update per environment step amortized over trajectory length, with no counterfactual rollouts and no verification rollouts. The cost per accepted improvement is exactly the cost of the original on-policy data; segments are mined offline from already-collected trajectories. A small replay buffer over `E(u, v)` retains witnesses as long as memory allows. At deployment, only `π_θ` is used; the multigraph is discarded.

7. **Nearest-neighbor novelty audit:** SIT (`worklogs/candidates/sit-suffix-inheritance-trie.md`, alive-weak) is the closest prior. SIT compares *suffixes diverging from a shared prefix node* and verifies grafts with a fresh rollout; RSD compares *segments converging to a shared endpoint node* and never verifies. The distinction is structural: convergent witness-pairs *share their downstream stochastic future by construction* (everything after `o_j` is identically distributed for both segments), so dominance interpretation does not need a verification rollout — the comparison is statistically clean a priori. SIT's bootstrap pathology was that suffixes from a node have heterogeneous futures; RSD eliminates that confound at the cost of needing *closed* witness pairs (segments that both end at the same observed node).

8. **Predicted failure modes:**
   - **Observation-hash collision rate too high:** if `h(o)` maps semantically distinct states to the same node, parallel edges no longer represent equivalent endpoints and dominance comparisons are spurious. Diagnostic: track average post-segment trajectory divergence between paired edges; if non-trivially nonzero, hash is too coarse.
   - **No reconvergence:** in environments where observations never repeat (high-entropy continuous control, irreversible state changes), `|E(u, v)| = 1` everywhere and the operator is a no-op. Will fail on Craftax-Symbolic if the symbolic state never recurs.
   - **Stochastic transitions:** parallel-edge dominance is an in-expectation Pareto comparison; under high noise, single-witness comparisons mislabel. Mitigation: require `|E(u, v)| ≥ N_min` before applying the operator. Failure if `N_min` cannot be reached in budget.
   - **Terminal-only reward with no auxiliary vector channel:** the appended step-cost channel reduces the operator to "imitate the shorter path between recurring observations." This is a real but weak signal — it should solve DoorKey-5x5/6x6 once the goal is hit once, but will not make the *first* goal hit. Collapses to shortest-path imitation in this corner.
   - **Vector channels all-zero except at terminal:** terminal vector is attributed to the terminal-emitting segment only; non-terminal segments compare on step-cost alone. Same degeneracy as above.

9. **Side-information channel:** {transition geometry, vector diagnostics}. Transition geometry is exposed via the observation-hash equivalence (which observations the agent actually revisits); vector diagnostics is the panel's `info["vector"]` plus an appended constant step-cost channel. No event lens, no demonstrations, no pretrained priors, no learned dynamics, no verifier.

10. **Monotonic improvement claim:** assume (i) `h(o)` induces a true equivalence on the downstream return distribution (post-`o_j` futures are identically distributed across paired segments), and (ii) per-step vector feedback is additive within a segment (it sums; this is by definition). Then for any `(u, v)` with two segments `s* ≻ s_-` (Pareto-dominant in coordinate-wise sense), replacing `s_-`'s policy probability mass with `s*`'s in expectation produces a coordinate-wise Pareto-improvement of the *segment-restricted* expected vector return between `u` and `v` — no channel decreases, at least one strictly increases. The global behavior policy improves segment-wise in Pareto sense; this is monotonic on the partial order over policies induced by per-segment vector accumulation, with no scalar projection at any step.

## Why it is not SIT

SIT (alive-weak candidate, nearest_prior=02-bric) compares *divergent suffixes* from a shared prefix observation and committed grafts via a fresh verification rollout — its structural problem is that suffixes from a shared prefix do *not* share their downstream environment, so a graft that succeeds once may fail on the next rollout, hence the verification step. RSD inverts the topology: it compares *convergent segments* between two shared-endpoint observation nodes, and the two segments by construction share both their upstream context (the agent's true history before `u` is reused) and their downstream future distribution (everything after `v`), so dominance is statistically valid without verification rollouts. The two methods both use observation-hash equivalence as the geometric primitive, but they exploit it on opposite sides of the trajectory and the witness statistics they rely on are different (SIT: divergent-suffix outcome distribution; RSD: convergent-segment within-window vector accumulation).

## Why it scales beyond the substrate

At horizon 20k actions with vector feedback (cost, validity, latency, safety, success, preference), trajectories almost certainly revisit "canonical observation contexts" — checkpointed file states in coding agents, "tool returned with status X" tokens in LLM-tool agents, repeated UI screen layouts in computer-use, repeated room layouts at long horizon. Each canonical revisit is a free counterfactual: two distinct action subsequences that produce the same downstream context, whose vector accumulations differ. RSD's primitive scales because the supply of natural counterfactuals grows roughly as `O(T^2 / |O|)` — quadratic in horizon — so longer episodes *strengthen* the primitive rather than weakening it (unlike value-bootstrap, which degrades as horizon grows). The action space being "generate a paragraph" or "call an API" is irrelevant to the operator: action sequences are stored as opaque tokens for the cloning target; only the observation hash (which can be a learned semantic embedding for unstructured envs) and the vector feedback are needed for dominance. Vector feedback is handled coordinate-wise from the start, so 6-channel feedback never collapses to a scalar — exactly what the modern long-horizon mission demands.
